Problem

Mailchimp needed to update its revenue model to match an expanding product suite. I led the design-research effort that informed how we would go on to display our product merchandising to our customers.

Mailchimp's product suite matured tremendously since its founding in 2000, but its pricing structure remained exactly the same. By the time my team was tasked with implementing the improved revenue model, our customers were getting a bargain on the product ‚Äî but they didn't always know what they were paying for.

Challenges

The new pricing model added a lot of complexity for our customers to understand. As with any new features, there was going to be a learning curve as customers learned about the new pricing model. We wanted to ensure that as our pricing model expanded, we added clarity over complexity.

This update to our pricing structure was also concurrent with a top-down refresh to Mailchimp's lovable brand. As I considered improvements to the functionality of this page, I also worked closely with the working team that was nailing down the specifics of our new patterns, color palettes, and components.

Solution

At the end of 2 rounds of user testing with customers and several tech spec talks with engineers, we landed on a page where the customer would make two decisions in sequence:

  1. Select your plan: Because different plans accommodate different ranges in the number of contacts, a slider loaded dynamically according to which plan was selected.
  2. Select your contact tier: As the customer adjusts the slider, the estimated monthly price updates accordingly.
This is how customers adjust their plan details in Mailchimp today. It's a far cry from the sweet little quasi-3-tiered system that was in place before, and it's been a critical step in scaling the business.
